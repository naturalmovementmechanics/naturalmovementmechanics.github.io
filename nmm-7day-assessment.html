Before you begin

This is not a quiz. It's a clinical conversation — with yourself.

Most people arrive at pelvic health care with a vague sense that something is off. This guide gives you the language and structure to get specific. That specificity matters — it shapes the care you receive, the questions you ask, and the outcomes you can expect.

Each day covers one domain. Answer honestly. Nothing here is embarrassing — it's all medical. The questions are adapted from validated clinical screening tools used in physical therapy practice.

01
One domain per day

Seven focused areas, each taking 2–3 minutes. One domain per day — observation over time tells you more than rushing through it all at once.

02
Answer honestly

No one is grading you. Underreporting is the most common barrier to good pelvic health care. The circle is empty — fill it accurately.

03
Bring this with you

Print or save this document and bring it to your first pelvic PT appointment. It will save 20 minutes of intake and give your clinician a meaningful starting point.

Important

This self-assessment is for educational awareness and does not constitute medical diagnosis. It is not a substitute for individualized clinical evaluation. If you are experiencing acute symptoms, pain, or significant concern, please seek care directly. Pelvic floor physical therapy has the potential to meaningfully improve function, reduce symptoms, and restore quality of life across a wide range of conditions — and in most situations, a skilled clinician can make a real and lasting difference. Assessment is always the essential first step.

Bladder Patterns

How is your bladder actually behaving?

Most people don't know what "normal" looks like. Today, you establish your baseline.

A typical bladder holds 300–600ml and signals fullness — not urgency — as it fills. Most people urinate 6–8 times per day and zero to once per night. If your patterns look different, that's useful clinical data, not something to be embarrassed about.

Dr. Alli's Clinical Note

Urinary frequency above 8 times/day and any nocturia beyond once are clinically significant. The "just in case" voiding pattern is one of the most common contributors to urgency — you're training your bladder to expect emptying at low volumes. This is addressable through bladder retraining. The triggers question is key for urgency urinary incontinence assessment.

What to notice
  • Going more than 8 times/day or more than once per night warrants clinical evaluation
  • Strong urgency that's hard to defer = overactive bladder pattern
  • Trigger-associated urgency = neurological-pelvic floor coordination issue
  • "Just in case" voiding = behavioral component that responds very well to retraining

Leakage & Control

The leaking you've been tolerating.

Leaking is common. It is not normal. Today you name what's happening so it can be treated.

There are three primary types of urinary incontinence: stress (leakage with physical pressure), urgency (leakage with the "gotta go" sensation), and mixed (both). Each has a distinct treatment pathway. Knowing your type matters clinically.

Dr. Alli's Clinical Note

The activity avoidance question is the one that tells me the true functional impact — not the leakage itself, but what it's costing you. Patients often underestimate this because they've adapted so gradually. "I just don't jump on trampolines anymore" or "I stopped running two years ago" — those are losses. They're also recoverable ones.

What to notice
  • Leakage with exertion = stress urinary incontinence → pelvic floor coordination + The Knack
  • Leakage with urgency = urgency urinary incontinence → bladder retraining + possible OAB treatment
  • Both = mixed incontinence → requires combined approach
  • Any activity you're avoiding because of leakage is a treatment goal worth naming explicitly

Pelvic Pain

Pain that was dismissed is still pain.

If you've been told your pelvic pain is normal, stress-related, or untreatable — today we look at it clearly.

Pelvic pain encompasses a broad territory: pain in the pelvic region at rest or with activity, pressure or heaviness, tailbone pain, pain with urination or bowel movements, pain with sitting, and pain with or after intercourse. All of these have clinical explanations and treatment pathways.

Dr. Alli's Clinical Note

Chronic pelvic pain (lasting more than 3 months) is one of the most underdiagnosed and undertreated conditions in physical therapy. If you've been told your pain is stress or anxiety, that may be a partial picture — but it is rarely the whole story. Pelvic floor hypertonicity, trigger points, neural sensitization, and connective tissue restrictions are all physical, measurable, and treatable. Your pain has a source.

What to notice
  • Any pain with sitting, intercourse, or urination is clinically significant regardless of duration
  • Tailbone pain frequently traces to pelvic floor muscle tension — not a bony injury
  • Low back + pelvic pain together often indicates integrated dysfunction (pelvic floor + lumbar + hip)
  • Duration matters: longer duration typically means more layers to address — but does not mean untreatable

Bowel Health

The half of pelvic health nobody talks about.

Bowel function is pelvic floor function. And it's rarely assessed — until today.

The pelvic floor coordinates both bladder and bowel function. Constipation creates sustained downward pressure that strains the pelvic floor over time. Fecal urgency and incomplete evacuation are both signs of pelvic floor incoordination. None of this is a digestion-only problem.

Dr. Alli's Clinical Note

Straining at stool and breath-holding during defecation are among the highest-load activities a pelvic floor encounters. Chronic straining is a significant contributor to prolapse progression, hemorrhoids, and pelvic pain. The mechanics of defecation — squatting position, relaxing rather than pushing, managing intra-abdominal pressure — are teachable. This is PT territory.

What to notice
  • Straining always or often = high load on the pelvic floor → posture, mechanics, and fiber/fluid need addressing
  • Incomplete evacuation + straining = possible pelvic floor dyssynergia (muscles contracting when they should relax)
  • Any fecal urgency or leakage = significant clinical finding → refer directly to pelvic PT
  • A squatting footstool under your feet can reduce straining substantially — a simple first intervention

Movement & Exercise

What your body avoids tells us a great deal.

Movement is both a context for symptoms and a treatment tool. Today we map both.

The pelvic floor is a load-bearing structure. How it performs — or fails to perform — during exercise, lifting, impact activity, and daily movement reveals its functional status. Avoidance of activity due to symptoms is both a quality-of-life measure and a clinical indicator.

Dr. Alli's Clinical Note

Pelvic heaviness or a bulging sensation during or after exercise is a classic prolapse symptom that often goes unreported because people assume it's normal. It is not. It warrants assessment. Separately — what you've stopped doing is often more important than what you're currently doing. Those activities are your treatment benchmarks.

What to notice
  • Leakage with impact = stress urinary incontinence → The Knack + progressive loading
  • Breath-holding during exercise = major pelvic floor loading risk → exhale on exertion
  • Pelvic heaviness with exercise = probable prolapse symptom → requires PT assessment
  • Activity avoidance → this becomes your primary functional goal in treatment

Intimacy & Sexual Function

This is medical. You are allowed to answer honestly.

Pain during intimacy is not a relationship problem. It's a pelvic floor problem — with a clinical solution.

Sexual health is a legitimate domain of physical therapy practice. Pain with intercourse (dyspareunia), inability to engage in intercourse (vaginismus), reduced sensation, postpartum changes to sexual function, and pain after intercourse are all assessable and treatable conditions. You are not expected to simply accept them.

Dr. Alli's Clinical Note

Superficial pain at entry typically indicates pelvic floor hypertonicity or tissue changes (including those from hormonal shifts or postpartum healing). Deep pain often involves internal muscle trigger points or organ-referral patterns. Both respond to manual therapy and targeted rehabilitation. The research on pelvic PT for dyspareunia and vaginismus is compelling — these are not conditions to simply endure.

What to notice
  • Any pain with intercourse = clinical finding regardless of duration or severity
  • Muscle-driven tightness (vaginismus pattern) → responds very well to pelvic PT + progressive desensitization
  • Deep pain + low back or hip pain = integrated dysfunction pattern
  • Postpartum changes to sexual function often go untreated for years — they shouldn't

Quality of Life

The real cost of what you've been carrying.

Today isn't about symptoms. It's about what those symptoms have cost you — and what you want back.

Clinical outcomes in pelvic health are not only measured in leakage frequency or pain scores. They're measured in whether someone went back to running, laughed without fear, stopped planning their day around bathroom locations, or slept through the night. Today we assess that dimension.

Dr. Alli's Clinical Note

Question 5 is the most important question in this entire assessment. That specific goal — running a 5K, playing with your kids on the floor, laughing freely, sleeping through the night — is the reason treatment matters. It's not about symptom reduction in the abstract. It's about that. Write it down. Bring it in. It becomes the north star of your care.

What to notice
  • Bathroom planning = functional impairment, not personal quirk — it has clinical significance
  • Anxiety about symptoms is common and valid; it also responds to treatment (symptom resolution reduces anxiety)
  • Duration without professional support is just a number — not a predictor of outcome. Treatment works regardless.
  • Your answer to question 5 is your treatment goal. It should be the first thing you say to your PT.
After your 7 days

Reading your results

This assessment doesn't produce a single score — pelvic health doesn't work that way. What it produces is a clinical picture across seven domains. Use the summary below to identify where your most significant findings cluster.

Low
Awareness Building

Minimal symptoms with limited functional impact. Pelvic health education and preventive practice are appropriate next steps.

Mod
Clinical Attention

Moderate symptoms with some functional limitation. A pelvic floor PT evaluation is strongly recommended to address patterns before they become entrenched.

High
Priority Care

Significant symptoms with functional impact across multiple domains. A pelvic PT evaluation and individualized treatment plan should be prioritized.
