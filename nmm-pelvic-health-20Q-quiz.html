Section 1 — The Plumbing Department
Question 01
The moment you put your key in the front door, does your bladder suddenly transform into a screaming toddler who cannot wait one more second and will not be negotiated with?
This is called "key-in-lock urgency" — and it's your bladder being a deeply dramatic roommate who signed no lease, pays no rent, and still makes all the rules. It's also very treatable.
Question 02
Do you leak when you sneeze, laugh, cough, jump, or — the holy trinity — all three in rapid succession at someone's birthday party?
Welcome to stress urinary incontinence, which affects roughly 1 in 3 women and approximately 100% of people who have never been told it's fixable. Spoiler: it's fixable. You don't have to retire from trampolines.
Question 03
Do you feel a heaviness, pressure, or "something is not where it used to be" sensation — especially after a long day on your feet?
Your pelvic organs are supported by a hammock of muscle and connective tissue. If that hammock has had a rough few years — pregnancy, heavy lifting, the general chaos of existing — it may need some attention. Not an emergency. But not nothing.
Question 04
Are you visiting the bathroom more than 8 times a day, or waking up at night more than once — when you'd really rather be unconscious?
Your bladder is trainable. It is not your boss. It just thinks it is — and it has been getting away with it because nobody told it otherwise. We can have that conversation.
Question 05
Do you strain, hold your breath, or find yourself genuinely negotiating with your bowels like they're a difficult contractor who sets their own timeline and answers to no one?
Constipation and pelvic floor dysfunction are far more connected than most people realize. Chronic straining puts real downward pressure on the very structures we're trying to support. If your bathroom visits have become a whole event requiring preparation and optimism, that's worth addressing.
Section 2 — Pain, Pressure & the Uninvited Guests
Question 06
Do you have pain or discomfort during sex, with tampon use, or with any internal pressure — the kind you've possibly just started quietly avoiding?
Pain with intimacy is genuinely common, wildly under-discussed, and often met with the world's least helpful response: "just relax." There are actual reasons this happens. There are actual solutions. You are not imagining it and you don't have to just get used to it.
Question 07
Do you have ongoing low back, hip, tailbone, or pelvic pain that hangs around like a houseguest who missed the hint that the visit ended three weeks ago?
Persistent pain in this region is almost always muscular, movement-related, or a coordination problem between structures that stopped talking to each other. That's good news — it means there's something to work with. Your body isn't broken; it's just holding a grudge.
Question 08
Do you have a scar from a C-section, episiotomy, or abdominal surgery that feels tight, numb, or pulls in ways that travel farther than you'd expect?
Scar tissue doesn't stay politely where it forms. It can adhere to surrounding layers and quietly pull on structures nowhere near the original site — contributing to bladder symptoms, pelvic pain, or low back tightness years later. This is one of the most underaddressed issues in women's health, full stop.
Section 3 — Movement, Strength & the Body You Live In
Question 09
Have you quietly stopped running, jumping, lifting, hiking, or doing something you used to love — because your body has made it more stressful than enjoyable?
Avoidance is a completely reasonable short-term strategy. It's also how people accidentally spend five years not jumping on trampolines. The goal isn't to push through — it's to actually fix the thing so you can stop managing around it.
Question 10
Do you feel like a specific area of your body is always braced, clenched, or "on guard" — even when you're just sitting there minding your own business?
Your body hired a bouncer after some incident it found threatening — and the bouncer never went home. Chronic muscle guarding is your nervous system being overly loyal. Sweet, but exhausting, and absolutely something that can be retrained.
Question 11
Do you spend most of your day either glued to a chair or standing without a break (no judgment — sedentary jobs are real and the modern world didn't ask your spine for input)?
Your hip flexors after a full day of sitting are basically a rubber band left in a hot car — technically still functional, technically not okay. Your body wants movement variety. It is not asking for a lot.
Section 4 — The Stuff Nobody Told You
Question 12
When you notice yourself standing, are you living in a forward collapse — chin jutting out, ribs dropped, tailbone tucked — like your skeleton is slowly trying to return to a seated position?
Posture isn't about standing up straight like your grandmother told you. It's about the relationship between your rib cage, pelvis, and the pressure system they manage together. A chronically collapsed or over-tucked posture changes how your pelvic floor has to work — and not in its favor.
Question 13
Do you hold your breath when you lift something heavy, brace hard at the gym, pick up a child, or move furniture — and only remember to exhale when you're lightheaded or someone reminds you?
Breath-holding during exertion spikes intra-abdominal pressure and sends that force straight down into the pelvic floor. Think of your pelvic floor less like a load-bearing wall and more like a trampoline — it needs coordinated breathing to manage that pressure properly. The cue is simple: exhale on effort. Every time.
Question 14
During exercises like sit-ups, crunches, or heavy lifting, do you notice a ridge, dome, or cone shape appearing down the center of your abdomen?
This can be a sign of diastasis recti — a separation of the abdominal muscles common postpartum and frequently mistaken for "just a belly." Doming is your body's way of saying "not yet" for that particular load — not "never." The rectus abdominis absolutely needs to be strengthened, but how you get there and in what sequence matters enormously. That's exactly what guidance is for.
Question 15
Do you wake up stiff, achy, or unable to find a comfortable position — and does your body seem to need a full reboot before it's willing to cooperate with the day?
Morning stiffness that lingers more than a few minutes is your body filing a formal complaint about how it's being loaded — or not moved — during the day. It's often joint irritation, muscle imbalance, or a nervous system running a little too hot. The mattress is rarely the actual villain here.
Question 16
Have your feet changed — flatter arches, less stability underfoot, or a sense that your balance just isn't quite what it used to be?
Feet are the foundation of the entire kinetic chain — and their relationship to the pelvis is more direct than most people realize. Pregnancy-related laxity can permanently alter arch structure and ankle stability, but even without pregnancy, flat feet affect the knees, hips, pelvis, and pelvic floor. Your feet are load-sharing partners in all of this. They deserve a little credit.
Section 5 — Life Has Happened to You
Question 17
Are you currently pregnant, in the first two years postpartum, or somewhere in the perimenopause-to-menopause stretch?
All three are enormous hormonal and biomechanical transitions. All three are dramatically under-supported by the healthcare system. Postpartum in particular: your body just built and launched an entire human being, and society's recovery plan was six weeks, a co-pay, and the suggestion to do Kegels.
Question 18
Have you had an injury, surgery, or significant physical or emotional event that your body seems to remember far more vividly than you'd like?
The body keeps receipts. This isn't woo — it's neuroscience. Old injuries, unresolved tension, and past trauma can absolutely show up as current physical symptoms. The good news: the body that kept the receipt can also cash it in for something better.
Section 6 — The Honest Reckoning
Question 19
Have you Googled your symptoms, received a shrug from a provider, and quietly decided this is just your life now — like a broken dishwasher you've learned to hand-wash around?
Acceptance is a virtue. Accepting unnecessary suffering as an inevitable consequence of having a body is not a plan — it's resignation with better vocabulary. You deserve actual options, not just better coping strategies.
Question 20
Honestly — are your symptoms making you smaller? Avoiding, hiding, managing, pre-planning, or just generally living around your body instead of in it?
Quality of life is a legitimate clinical outcome. If your body is running the itinerary — deciding where you go, what you wear, what you agree to — that's information worth acting on. You should be the one in charge of your schedule. Not your pelvic floor.
