What is pelvic health, actually?

It's more than you think. It's more than you were told.

Pelvic health physical therapy is the specialized clinical assessment and treatment of the muscles, fascia, ligaments, nerves, and joints of the pelvic region. When these structures aren't working well — too tight, too weak, or poorly coordinated — the effects ripple into bladder control, bowel function, pain, intimacy, movement, and quality of life.

Your pelvic floor is 14+ muscles working as a coordinated system. They support your organs, control bladder and bowel function, contribute to sexual response, and connect your trunk to your lower body. This challenge focuses on two of the most common presentations: stress urinary incontinence and pelvic organ prolapse. Both are treatable. Both respond to the techniques you'll learn here.

Who this challenge is designed for

Two common conditions. Both very treatable.

Stress Urinary Incontinence

Leaking urine when pressure increases suddenly.

Coughing, sneezing, laughing, jumping, or lifting can all trigger leakage when the pelvic floor muscles lack the coordination or strength to manage the sudden rise in intra-abdominal pressure. This is the most common type of incontinence — and one of the most responsive to targeted exercise and technique training.

Pelvic Organ Prolapse

When pelvic organs descend due to weakened support structures.

The bladder, uterus, or rectum can shift downward when the muscles, ligaments, and connective tissue that hold them in place are weakened — through childbirth, hormonal changes, chronic straining, or simply time. Symptoms often include pelvic heaviness, pressure, or a bulging sensation. Prolapse is graded by severity; many people with prolapse can return to full activity with proper pelvic floor rehabilitation.

Important · More severe prolapse

If your prolapse is more advanced, strengthening exercises may feel uncomfortable or create pressure — a sign that the pelvic floor may be contracting into the prolapse itself. Do not push through pain or heaviness. This is a signal to seek a formal assessment from a pelvic floor PT before continuing. Graded, supervised rehabilitation is available — and often effective — at every severity level.

Day 1
Awareness · Pelvic Floor Contraction (Kegel)
Find Your Floor

Before you can strengthen something, you have to find it. And before you can find it, you have to decide it's worth looking for.

The pelvic floor is one of the few muscle groups most people have never been formally introduced to. It sits like a hammock at the base of the pelvis, and it's doing real work every single moment — even when you're not thinking about it. Today, we're going to make that relationship conscious.

We start seated — not because that's where life happens, but because it's where the nervous system can finally pay attention without distraction. Seated work is how we establish the connection. Everything after this builds on what you find today.

PT Insight

In clinical practice, I often see people "gripping" when they're asked to activate their pelvic floor — they hold their breath, tighten their glutes, and brace their abs. That's not a kegel. That's a whole-body stress response. Today, we interrupt that pattern. And we start seated on purpose — it reduces postural demand and lets you actually feel what you're looking for before we ask those muscles to work in the positions life actually demands.

Today's Practice
  1. Find a comfortable seated position. Feet flat on the floor, spine relaxed (not slumped). Take three slow breaths to arrive.
  2. Imagine a blueberry sitting at the base of your pelvic floor. Without holding your breath or squeezing your glutes, gently lift that blueberry upward. Hold for 3 seconds. Release completely. This gentle lift-and-release — isolated, controlled, without breath-holding or glute gripping — is what a correct pelvic floor contraction (a kegel) actually feels like. If you've been doing something different, you're not alone. Most people are.

  4. Now try a fast-twitch version: quick flick up and fully release. Do 10 of these. This trains the muscles that respond to sudden pressure — sneezing, laughing, jumping.
  5. Finish with 3 full diaphragmatic breaths. Notice how the pelvic floor moves with your breath without any effort from you.

Day 2
Education · Reframe
Unlearn the Shame

You didn't learn to be embarrassed about your body spontaneously. It was taught. Today, we unteach some of it.

Pelvic dysfunction is extraordinarily common. Research consistently shows that 1 in 3 women experience urinary incontinence. Pelvic pain affects millions. Postpartum complications are routinely undertreated. And yet — most people wait years before seeking help, because they were told it was "normal," or they were too embarrassed to bring it up.

Today's practice is partly movement, partly mindset. Because you cannot fully rehabilitate a body you're ashamed of.

PT Insight

The nervous system responds to shame the same way it responds to threat. When patients are embarrassed during assessment, their pelvic floor tone changes — they guard, brace, and tighten. Shame literally interferes with healing. This isn't woo-woo. It's neuroscience.

Today's Practice
  1. Write down one thing about your pelvic health you've never said out loud — or have felt embarrassed about. You don't have to share it. Just name it.
  2. Below it, write: "This is a medical condition. It has a cause and a treatment. My body deserves care."
  3. Now move: Seated or standing, perform 10 slow hip circles in each direction. To do this: place your hands on your hips and slowly trace a large circle with your pelvis — forward, out to one side, back, and around to the other side, as if you were gently stirring with your hips. Keep your upper body relatively still. Reverse direction after 10 rotations. Feel your pelvis moving in space — fluid, not rigid. Reclaim the territory. Hip circles gently mobilize the sacroiliac joints and lumbar spine, increase circulation to the pelvic region, and reconnect you to your pelvis as something you can move intentionally. That shift in ownership matters clinically — and it is the foundation for the strength work that follows.
    Movement · Hip Circles — Pelvis Traces a Full Circle · Upper Body Stays Still
    Hands on hips · breathe freely
    ○trace the circle
    Pelvis leads the circle · ribs stay quiet
    →
    Pelvis leads · ribs stay quiet
    ·upper body still
  4. Add diaphragmatic breathing: inhale 4 counts through the nose (feel the belly, sides, and back expand), exhale slowly through pursed lips for 6 counts. Do 8 rounds.
  5. Finish by placing your hands on your lower abdomen. Take one breath toward your hands. You are allowed to care for this part of your body.
  6. Two more movements that belong in every pelvic floor program: bridging. Bridging works in an anti-gravity position — when your hips are lifted, gravity no longer pushes down on the pelvic organs, making this ideal for anyone managing prolapse symptoms. It also directly strengthens the posterior chain (glutes and hamstrings), which shares the mechanical load with your pelvic floor in standing, walking, and impact activities. A strong posterior chain is a supported pelvic floor. Note: if you feel pelvic heaviness, pressure, or discomfort during bridging, stop and note it. This is information for your PT — not a reason to stop pursuing care.

  7. Now add an adductor squeeze: place a rolled towel, small pillow, or soft ball between your knees. As you bridge up, gently squeeze it. The inner thighs (adductors) co-contract with the deep pelvic floor and hip stabilizers. For many people, the adductor connection is an easier neural pathway to the pelvic floor than isolated kegels alone — making this a clinically useful progression, not just a harder bridge.

Day 3
Technique · Coordination
The Knack

One technique. Clinically proven. You'll use it every day for the rest of your life once you have it.

The Knack is a pre-contraction strategy — you briefly and firmly contract the pelvic floor in the half-second before a sudden increase in intra-abdominal pressure (coughing, sneezing, laughing, jumping). Research shows it can reduce or eliminate stress urinary incontinence with consistent use.

This is not about white-knuckling your way through life. It's about training your nervous system to do this automatically, so you're not managing leakage — you've addressed it.

Notice how today's practice moves deliberately from seated to standing to impact. That's not accidental — it mirrors the progression from controlled training to real-world demand. You sneeze standing up. You laugh on your feet. You jump from the ground. If you only ever practice lying down or sitting, you're training a skill in a context that doesn't match the moment it's needed.

PT Insight

The Knack changed outcomes in my practice dramatically when I started teaching it consistently. It bridges the gap between isolated kegel exercises and real-life function. The position progression matters too — seated is where we learn, standing is where we confirm, impact is where we prove it. Each layer asks a little more of the system, which is exactly how you build durable, functional strength rather than just gym-floor performance.

Today's Practice
  1. Seated, practice a single pelvic floor contraction (from Day 1). Make sure you can do a firm, clean contraction with a full release. Confirm this 5 times. We start seated because it gives you the best sensory feedback — gravity is neutral, your postural muscles aren't working hard, and your nervous system can focus.
  2. Still seated, practice with a simulated cough: take a breath, tighten your pelvic floor firmly, then cough. Notice you stayed dry. Do this 5 times.

    ✓PF lifted ✓
    Seated, focused
    →
    ↓ Then cough — PF holds
    ·cough!
    ✓stays dry ✓
    Dry. Every time.
  3. Now move to standing. Repeat the simulated cough with a pre-contraction. Feel how the demand changes upright — gravity is now loading the system, which is exactly what happens in real life. Do 5 more. Standing is where most leakage actually happens. This is why we don't stay seated forever.
  4. Still standing, add a small hop: tighten before your feet leave the ground, land, release. Repeat 8 times. Impact loading is the highest demand you'll ask of the pelvic floor in daily life. If it works here, it works on the trampoline, at the gym, and on the running trail.
  5. Try three real-life simulations: a laugh (seriously — laugh), a cough, and a fake sneeze, each with a pre-contraction. Do them standing. Own the moment.
  6. Breathe it out. Remind yourself that this is a learnable skill, not a personality trait.

Day 4
Integration · Movement
Connect the Whole System

The pelvic floor doesn't work in isolation. It never has. Today, we bring the whole team into the room.

Your pelvic floor works in concert with your diaphragm, deep abdominals (transversus abdominis), and deep spinal muscles. Together, they form the pressure management system of your trunk. When one piece is dysregulated, the others compensate — and compensation, over time, becomes dysfunction.

Today's practice coordinates the whole system — breath, core, and pelvic floor — through movement. And again, we move through positions deliberately: we begin with the breath pattern in a low-demand posture, then take it upright, then add load. By the time you're doing a wall squat while managing your breath and your pelvic floor, you're doing something that looks a lot like picking up a child, carrying groceries, or getting up from a chair — because functionally, it is.

PT Insight

One of the most common errors I see is breath-holding during any kind of exertion. This creates massive increases in intra-abdominal pressure that the pelvic floor has to absorb. Teaching patients to exhale on exertion is a game-changer — for leakage, for prolapse symptoms, and for back pain. And the reason we practice it in standing and squatting positions — not just lying on a mat — is because that's where you live. Your pelvic floor needs to perform in your actual life, not just in your exercise hour.

Today's Practice
  1. Seated or standing, practice the "exhale on exertion" principle: inhale to prepare, exhale as you do the work. Try this with a standing row motion using your arms — reach forward with arms straight, then exhale as you pull back, keeping your elbows close and gliding along your ribcage. Do 10 reps, focusing on the breath pattern. This is the foundation of everything — learning to breathe through effort rather than brace against it.
    Exercise · Standing Row — Exhale on Pull
    ↑ Inhale — prepare

  2. Move to a standing wall squat: back against the wall, feet hip-width. Inhale as you lower, exhale and gently engage pelvic floor as you rise. 10 reps. If you need extra support or confidence starting out, stand in front of a sturdy chair or piece of furniture and hold the back lightly — this lets you focus entirely on the breath and pelvic floor pattern without worrying about balance. Standing and loading — this is the position in which you carry things, climb stairs, and stand from a chair. Training here means the skill transfers directly to those moments.

Day 5
Confidence · Next Steps
You Are Not Starting Over. You Are Starting Right.

Five days ago, you were reading a description. Now you're someone who has done the work. That's not a small thing.

Today's practice is a full integration — a short sequence that pulls together everything from this week: awareness, breath, the Knack, coordination, and the mindset shift that started on Day 1.

Look at what you've actually done this week. Day 1: you found your pelvic floor in a seated position. Day 2: you mobilized with hip circles and built strength through bridging — working anti-gravity to reduce load on the pelvic organs, then adding an adductor squeeze to deepen pelvic floor recruitment. Day 3: you took the Knack into standing, then impact. Day 4: you loaded the whole system upright, squatting and reaching with breath and pelvic floor working in coordination. That arc from supine → seated → standing → loaded → impact is exactly how pelvic floor rehabilitation progresses in a clinical setting. You didn't just do exercises. You trained a system across the positions your real life actually uses.

But first: a word about what comes next. Five days builds a foundation. It does not resolve years of dysfunction. If you've been dealing with leakage, pain, urgency, or prolapse symptoms, you deserve an actual clinical assessment from a pelvic floor physical therapist. Not because you failed this challenge — but because you now know enough to ask the right questions.

PT Insight

The bravest thing my patients ever do is make the appointment. Not the exercises — those come later. The bravest thing is deciding they deserve to be assessed. That's what I'm asking you to do next. You have over 20 years of my clinical experience behind that recommendation.

Today's Full Practice

  2. 2 minutes: 10 plain bridges + 10 bridges with adductor squeeze. Exhale on the rise. Anti-gravity, posterior chain, pelvic floor. Day 2's work, carried forward.
  3. 2 minutes: Isolated pelvic floor contractions — slow holds (5 count) and fast-twitch (10 reps). Full release between each. Do these seated first, then standing.
  4. 3 minutes: The Knack with simulated coughs, a jump, and a laugh — standing. (Yes, actually laugh.) On Day 1 you were seated. Today you're upright, laughing, and staying dry. That's functional progress.
